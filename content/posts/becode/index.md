# One Month of Becode

In early February 2026, I began a seven month bootcamp on AI and Data Science with BeCode. 
These are my thoughts after a little over a month. 

## Strap in

The pace is *fast*. 
I was excited to begin, full of energy, batteries charged and thermos full of tea, and then we spent the first day fixing the internet, admin, and setting up discord.
Not the epic start I was hoping for.

That leisurely pace lasted one day; on day two, the rocket launched, hard.\
Six weeks later, I write this after deploying my first machine learning model, that I trained on real estate data that I scrapped, cleaned, and analysed.
It’s a group project, so I didn’t do everything but still, that’s *fast*.\
I knew some python going in, I’d written functions that worked and occasionally did useful things, but this was on an different level: an actual program, solving a plausible need, that could be genuinely useful to someone. It had scripts, functions, helper functions, all spread in different files, tying together into a functioning whole. 

We were expected to have some basic python knowledge to start, but I would raise that requirement by quite a bit, at least if you want to *thrive* in the training and not just survive it. The course doesn’t teach as much as it tells you what to build and what you’ll need to learn to build it. You get a structure, projects and other people to do it with, but that’s it.

There are, of course, pros and cons to this approach.


## The good
Becode believe in the active pedagogy and so do I. 
The idea is to learn by doing rather than studying. At Becode, that means trying to achieve a goal, usually in the form of a project, which exposes gaps in knowledge and understanding, which forces to learn enough about it to overcome.
Progress is filling enough of those gaps along the way to cross the finish line. How many gaps and how you fill them is up to it, but if you can get the result, does it matter that much?

There is some theory provided, but short and to the point. Learning comes from drills, exercises, and projects.   

Learning like this forces confrontation with reality, actually testing understanding against it. The python interpreter can’t be swayed: if it crashes, the code is wrong. An active pedagogy fosters proactive independence, problem solving, and a ‘just do it’ attitude. Towards that, the more ambitious and well chosen the goal, the better: there is more learning to be done.  

For me, it’s a great motivator.\
Being given an objective while surrounded by motivated people also trying to reach that objective, I find it impossible not to get really into it. I work my ass off, push myself, and take the direct route to the goal. I learn what I need, implement, and keep going. 

It’s not perfect, but it is *fast*, and going fast gives impressive results. Within a few weeks, I was writing whole scripts, bigger functions than I’d ever written before, that depended on other functions, sometimes written by others and it *had* to work.\

Before the course, I would have thought I was *months* away from those kinds of projects.

Fun is another element that came as a surprise. 
Tackling ambitious projects, however messily, is fun and *succeeding* at them is energising. It’s plain *awesome* to see a terminal printing how many pages I’m scraping from a website, watching that number go up and up, *fast* because... well, you’re going implement multithreading right? Everybody else is doing it!

There are twenty two of us, tackling projects in group of 3-4, and seeing another group doing something cool is a challenge, an invitation.
Whether fomo, envy or emulation is at play, the result is the same: I pushed myself harder because others are smart too. 

## The bad.
Going fast has a price.\
Mainly, I sometimes get the impression of *skimming* rather than learning, that I could be missing an important concept and not even know about it. Traditional courses are designed to avoid this, to check whether a student has the minimum knowledge to pass the class, but there are no tests of any kind at Becode. 
Personally, I keep a list of topics I want explore further... but we go so fast, it’s hard to find the time. 

For instance, we were analysing a dataset of real estate properties we had previously scrapped. All fine, we just did a few days on pandas; now that I’m putting it to use... and realise I don’t know half of what I need to know. Not that I’m completely lost, but the pace is fast so I forgot a lot of what I just studied, didn’t fully understand the implications of some things, and misunderstood key concepts.

It’s not just syntax either; methods I practiced get mixed up with others, concepts I thought I understood are trickier in practice. For pandas this manifested as problems with data types and shapes : concepts that are fundamental, but easy to skip over during drills. 

That means I regularly relied on LLM’s for help, reminders, or even understanding how to do what I’m trying to do. That feels like being a passenger more than a driver. Things get done, but am I doing them? 

Skimming material is not the same as studying it, but studying material is not the same as practicing it either. The fundamental Becode opinionated gamble is that *practice is study*. Trip over the same roadblock enough time, and it will eventually ‘click’. This can feel like a roundabout way of going about it though: why learn something badly once, only to relearn it later? It’s simpler and more logical to do things right the first time. 

Another problem is the quality of the study material itself: while not always bad, but it’s rarely excellent. It’s full of bad explanations, some downright mistakes, and not always very clear.
In addition, years of school have conditioned me to trust the study material, so it still feels weirds to abandon a bad explanation in the course to look for a good one elsewhere. My default assumption is that the material is hard, and I should power through it. But ometimes the explanation is just bad, and a good one makes it clear immediately.

Becode *encourages* looking for learning ressources, but for me the long ingrained default is still to study the material provided, not to find good material and study that.
Add the time pressure and it can get annoying trying to understand a convoluted series of messy paragraphs when an LLM gives better explanations instantly.

Now to be clear, those are problems and they are real, but it’s not always bad, far from it. 

## On Balance
The metric I use to evaluate Becode is time. 
I want to learn data science. To do that, is there a better way to spend my time that following this training? 


### What did I learn?

More that I can conveniently sum up. I could list tools but for me, the biggest teaching moments were relating to process, structure, architecture, design. Those are the result of *experience*: the way software engineering works, mediated by how *I* like to do it.

There is important learning happening when attempting hard things under time pressure, about needing to understand the core methods and surfaces of a new library, about needing to make a plan and try it out, *now*. 
I learned how to move ahead, how to structure work, and I’m developing an instinct for how to design software, about what needs to be solved first, and which problems can be put aside until a solution is needed, if at all. 

I saw that code is not written top down and linearly; it’s built in layers and modules. Often, a part needs to be discarded or tweaked: not only is that normal, it’s an expected part of the process. Build a part, test it, add features and complexity to it. Test that, and keep going.
Some of the code will change, but a working part is better than a broken whole. The code is a tool to get a job done. I like crafting it, but if it’s not useful, so be it.   

None of this is new, but learning it in real time is satisfying, and those lessons were not in the books and courses that I studied. 

This is the key: maybe you don’t acquire as much knowledge with a bootcamp as with a course, but you acquire something much more valuable: *competence*. 

### Yay or nay?
A bootcamp makes an implicit promise: you will get out of it what you are capable of putting into it. If you go in weak in knowledge, motivation or resourcefulness, the pace and the ambition of the projects will be hard to deal with.

I came in *somewhat* prepared and very hungry. The challenges taught me more than anything else I’d tried before and faster than I thought possible. 

The bootcamp simulates a fast paced working environment: here is a task, a team, get it done. Figure out what you’ll need to do, what you’ll need to know to do it. The documentation sucks, and the presentation is due on Friday, so get to it.

If you’re thinking about doing a bootcamp, prepare and hold on tight. 
I don’t know of a better way to learn: it’s hard and that’s good.
